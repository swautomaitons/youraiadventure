Analysis

This week's AI news comes with a lesson in the economics of "unlimited."

Anthropic blocked 135,000+ OpenClaw users from flat-rate plans effective April 4. Some affected users are reporting cost increases of up to 50x. The official explanation from Boris Cherny, Head of Claude Code: "Our subscriptions weren't built for the usage patterns of these third-party tools."

For those of us who've been watching the AI pricing landscape, this was predictable — if not inevitable.

Autonomous agent workflows consume 10–100x more tokens than manual human-in-the-loop usage. A $20/month "unlimited" subscription absorbs that cost up to a point. Beyond that point, the economics break. The only question was when, not if.

What this signals for anyone building on AI platforms:

1. Flat-rate pricing for compute-intensive workloads is a temporary arrangement, not a durable business input. Model it as a variable, not a fixed cost.

2. The more capable and autonomous AI becomes, the less viable flat-rate subscriptions are. This is a structural tension, not a one-off event.

3. Diversification matters. Open-source models (Gemma 4, Llama 4) are increasingly viable — and they don't have this repricing risk.

The concessions Anthropic offered are reasonable: a one-time credit and a discount on pre-purchased bundles. They don't change the fundamental math.

OpenAI, Google, xAI — all running versions of this same model. Anthropic just went first publicly. Worth watching how the others handle it.
