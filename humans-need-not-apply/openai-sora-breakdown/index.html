Analysis

OpenAI shut down Sora. Here's the business case for why it was inevitable.

The numbers: $1 million per day in compute costs. Under 500,000 active users at shutdown. A $1 billion Disney licensing deal covering 200+ characters — cancelled. Disney's CEO got less than one hour's notice.

Altman's statement was unusually direct: "We need to concentrate our compute and our product capacity into these next generation of automated researchers and companies."

The strategic context matters here. Enterprise revenue now represents over 40% of OpenAI's $2 billion monthly revenue run rate. GPT-5.5 — internally called "Spud" — just completed pretraining. The resource reallocation isn't speculation. It's already happening.

What this signals for the broader AI market:

1. Consumer AI tools without enterprise revenue paths are structurally exposed 2. Compute allocation is the most honest signal of a company's actual strategy — not the press releases 3. Studio and media IP licensing deals with AI companies carry significant termination risk 4. The "land with consumers, sell to enterprise" playbook only works if the consumer product generates meaningful data or distribution value

The math on Sora never closed. $1M/day against sub-500K active users is approximately $2/user/day — for a free-tier product. No monetization model covers that gap.

This isn't a failure story. It's a prioritization story. OpenAI made the right call, just later than the data suggested they should have.
